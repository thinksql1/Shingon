Lesson 1: The Origins and Journey of Shingon Buddhism

From India to Japan, a Secret Teaching Unfolds

Indian Origins: The Roots of Mikkyō

Shingon Buddhism, known in Japanese as Mikkyō ("secret teaching"), is a form of Esoteric Buddhism that developed gradually in India. Its origins can be traced to a synthesis of various doctrines, rituals, and meditation techniques. Early Indian culture, including Brahmanism, contributed practices like the fire ritual (goma), which was later adapted into esoteric Buddhist practice.

With the rise of Mahayana ("Great Vehicle") Buddhism around the end of the first century C.E., new concepts like the bodhisattva emerged, who seeks enlightenment for all beings. This movement included important ritual and devotional aspects that were foundational to the development of Esoteric Buddhism. Over time, these practices, combined with Mahayana philosophy, formed a coherent system.

The Journey to China: The Great T'ang Patriarchs

Esoteric Buddhist teachings were first introduced to China from Central Asia, where their magical elements resonated with local shamanistic beliefs. However, it was during the T'ang dynasty (618-907) that the "pure" esoteric teachings were systematically transmitted. This era was marked by the arrival of three great masters from India:

  • Shubhakarasimha (637-735): Arrived in China in 716 and brought the foundational text, the Dainichi-kyō. He was welcomed by the emperor and performed many rituals at the court.
  • Vajrabodhi (671-741): Arrived around 720 and introduced important texts from the Kongōchō-gyō lineage, laying a strong foundation for esoteric teachings in China. He is considered the fifth Shingon patriarch.
  • Amoghavajra (705-774): The disciple of Vajrabodhi and the sixth patriarch, he traveled back to India and Ceylon to gather over five hundred volumes of texts. He was highly influential, serving three successive T'ang emperors and is recognized as one of China's four great Buddhist translators.

Timeline of Esoteric Buddhism's Development

~1500-1200 B.C.E.

Aryan invasion of India; early Brahmanistic rituals like the fire ceremony take form.

~1st Century C.E.

Mahayana Buddhism begins to rise, introducing key concepts like the bodhisattva and incorporating devotional rituals.

7th Century C.E.

The "pure" esoteric sutras, the Dainichi-kyō and Kongōchō-gyō, are compiled in India, marking the middle period of Mikkyo development.

716-720 C.E.

The great masters Shubhakarasimha and Vajrabodhi arrive in T'ang China, systematically introducing the core esoteric teachings.

Early 9th Century C.E.

The Japanese priest Kūkai travels to China, studies under the master Hui-kuo, and brings the complete esoteric teachings back to Japan, establishing the Shingon sect.

Kūkai and the Establishment of Shingon in Japan

The final and most crucial step in this journey for Japan was undertaken by the priest Kūkai (774-835). In 805, while studying in Ch'ang-an, the capital of T'ang China, he met the master Hui-kuo. Hui-kuo, the seventh patriarch, recognized Kūkai's potential and transmitted the entire esoteric doctrine of both the Dainichi-kyō and Kongōchō-gyō traditions to him in just three months.

Upon his return to Japan, Kūkai systematized these doctrines and practices, founding the Shingon sect. For over a thousand years, these teachings were kept secret, transmitted directly from master to pupil in an unbroken lineage. This marks the successful transmission of Mikkyō to Japan, where it has survived and flourished to the present day.
